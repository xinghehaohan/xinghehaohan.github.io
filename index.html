Risk Assessment
Main Challenges — In Order of Severity
1
Critical

No final deed — and probably never will be soon

The konvenju is 23 years old and was contingent on the Government acquiring the underlying land. The Government has admitted it cannot identify the landowner. What you would be buying is not registered ownership — it is a promise of sale plus possession. A Maltese notary may refuse to publish a deed of sale on this basis. A bank will almost certainly not lend against it. If the real landowner surfaces, they can challenge your position.

2
Critical

The paper trail breaks at Maria's death

The Housing Authority and Public Registry still list Luigia Sultana (d. 2007) as counterparty on file RTB 117/2002. Neither Luigia's inheritance to Maria (2007) nor Maria's inheritance to her children (2019) has been published as a causa mortis deed. Until those are done, the seller has no paper standing to sell anything.

3
High

Multiple heirs must all sign

Maltese succession law vests the estate equally in all of Maria's legitimate children. Every single one of them must sign the sale deed or grant a formal power of attorney. If one sibling is abroad, estranged, or uncooperative, the deal cannot complete. You do not yet know how many siblings there are.

4
High

Housing Authority may claim a clawback

The Sir Sid Darek scheme required the flat to be the beneficiary's ordinary residence. Maria died at St Vincent de Paule nursing home in Luqa, suggesting she was not residing at 102 Triq il-Punent during her final years. The Authority may demand refund of the Lm 6,500 subsidy revalued at today's market prices — potentially €30,000+. This must be resolved before you agree a price.

5
Medium

Likely emphyteusis, not freehold

The 2002 letter to the Commissioner of Land refers to ground rent, strongly suggesting the land is government-owned and the arrangement is a perpetual emphyteusis with an annual ground rent in perpetuity. Normal in Malta and saleable — but you need the figure before agreeing a price.

Recommended Actions
What to Do — In Order
1

Engage your own Maltese notary now — not the seller's

Agree fees upfront. Do not sign anything and do not pay any deposit until your notary has completed their own independent searches.

2

Have your notary write directly to the Housing Authority

Quoting file reference RTB 117/2002, ask: is the konvenju still live; has the Government acquired title to the land; what is the Authority's current position on the scheme's residency requirements; what subsidy refund will they apply on an onward sale; and will they approve a transfer at this stage at all.

3

Ask the seller to produce succession paperwork

Maria Scicluna's Article 29 Public Registry will-search certificate; any will she made; a complete list of every one of Maria's children with ID numbers; and any causa mortis deeds already published for either Luigia's or Maria's successions. If these don't exist, the seller — at her cost — must have a notary draft and publish them before you talk about price.

4

Commission an independent valuation from a Maltese architect (perit)

Not the seller's. This tells you what the property is worth assuming clean title — then you subtract a substantial discount for everything that isn't clean.

5

If and only if all of the above comes back cleanly — structure a protected konvenju

Any konvenju you sign should make every outstanding issue a condition precedent to the final deed, with full refund of any deposit if conditions are not met within a fixed window. Your notary drafts this, not the seller's.

Document-by-Document Analysis
Revised Assessment of the Original Checklist

1. "Agreement between aunt and father" (1992)

Likely Non-Existent
This document almost certainly does not exist — and the premise behind it is wrong. Luigia never transferred anything to any "father" in 1992. She remained the Housing Authority's tenant, personally applied to Sir Sid Darek in 2001, personally signed the konvenju in 2002, and left everything to her sister Maria by will in 2007. The seller either misremembered or is referring to an informal tenancy arrangement with no legal bearing. Still worth asking the seller what she meant — the answer is diagnostic of how well she understands her own file — but stop treating this as a document that needs to surface.

2. "POS from 2003"

On File — 2002
You have this. It is from 2002, not 2003: the Konvenju RTB 117/2002, signed 22 October 2002 before Notary Schembri Adami. The Batch 1 copy (7-page PDF) is the complete version. SCAN 2 is abridged and missing the hypothec and rental-termination clauses — work from Batch 1 only.

3. Housing Authority correspondence

Partial — Post-2002 Missing
You have the 11 November 2002 Housing Authority letter to the Commissioner of Land. What you don't have is anything after that. The seller's brief mentioned "several letters exchanged" and "the government updated that it could not locate the owner of the land." That correspondence trail is the single most important thing still missing on the HA side — it tells you exactly how stuck the file is and why. Ask the seller for the complete correspondence file: every letter in and every letter out.

4. Site plans

Missing
Not yet produced. Ask specifically for: the Planning Authority (PA) site plan for 102 Triq il-Punent, Valletta, and any architect's drawings showing unit boundaries. Your independent valuer will need these, and your notary will want to verify the footprint matches what's being sold. Note: Konvenju Clause 13 mentions common access, shared stairs, and excludes the roof — the exact scope of what you'd be buying depends on these plans.

5. Succession documents — Luigia & Maria

Critical — Missing
Still needed: Luigia's original 1983 will in certified form (from Notary Joseph Cachia's acts or the Notarial Archives); Luigia's causa mortis deed (estate passing to Maria, 2007); Maria's Public Registry will search (Art. 29 certificate); Maria's Civil Court search for any secret will; Maria's will itself (if any); and Maria's causa mortis deed (estate passing to her heirs, 2019). If either causa mortis deed was never published, those must be done — at the seller's cost — before you can proceed.

6. Who signs the deed?

Unresolved
The answer on paper is: every single one of Maria Scicluna's heirs must sign. It is not a matter of one family member being designated "the seller." Unless there is a formal power of attorney from the other heirs to one of them, all are required at the deed. You need: the full list of Maria's heirs with IDs and current addresses; confirmation that none are minors or deceased with their own sub-heirs; confirmation all are willing to sell (in writing); and whether one will act by POA or all will appear in person. Until you have these — in writing, verifiable against succession paperwork — you have a single family member who says she speaks for everyone. This is the question that most often kills Maltese inherited-property deals.
